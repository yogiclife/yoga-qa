**Original Question:**  
Clarification by acharya ji.

**Full Answer:**  
After hearing your description of pashu dharma and manava dharma as well as that of animals in human form and humans in human form, X ji became concerned and said that as a child she lived in a lower middle class family, and they were always occupied with thinking about and trying to manage where their next meal would come from, and if they were able to get two meals in a day they were very happy. And she asked you whether this was pashu jiivan, living a life as animal in human form. And you reassured her that this was not animal life, this was what was needed at that time, and that fulfillment of basic needs -- food, clothing, shelter, education, medical care -- these are basic needs which one needs to fulfill. And it is only when one takes more than one needs of these things that it becomes animal life. So if one is over-eating or overaccumulating money etc, then that is animal life for a human being as they are trying to fulfill their infinite desire using finite things. 

So this is an interesting point which you raise, and I would like to address it to make it clear. I understand why you wanted to reassure X ji, for her family was not doing anything wrong. They were doing what they had to do; they were poor and they needed to survive. 

Here I would like to separate the matter into two parts:

1. The impoverished people of the world have essentially no option but to follow animal life. They are forced to do so by their existential circumstances. And it is not their fault; it is the natural outcome of capitalist society that a very few will be very rich, and the great majority will be quite poor. As such, they have no option but to focus on their survival: food, clothing, shelter. And that is what animals do. No human wants to be poor; they are poor because they have been exploited by the few who are extremely rich. This is a defect at the collective level, the societal level. X ji's family was not at fault; but still, it has to be said that their life was animal life. That is what animals do in the wild, right? They struggle just to survive. Animals' lives are all about physical survival, and so it was with X ji's family. Manava dharma means leading a life focused on realization of the Infinite Entity. Atmamoksa and jagat-hita. And X ji's family was in no position to do either of these; so it was animal life indeed, although it was not at all their fault-- it was the fault of society.

2. The second part of this matter regards those who have plenty of money or, at least, sufficient money, and still focus their lives on food and other forms of bhoga, sense-gratification. These are the people who abuse animal dharma, trying to satisfy the infinite desire within them by accumulation of finite things. As the above group in part 1, these people in part 2 are also animals in human form-- but in this case they are themselves at fault whereas in the case of impoverished people, society is at fault.

This is not to say that if provided the financial means, all the impoverished people in the world would start following manava dharma; indeed even then, most would not-- but some would. Without allowing them to have their basic needs met, we are not even giving them a chance.

So both groups-- those in part 1 and those in part 2-- are animals in human form. Anyone who is not organizing their life around atmamoksa and jagaddhita is an animal in human form. It needs realization that one has within an infinite desire that can only be satisfied by realization of the Infinite Entity and to begin taking action on that realization-- then only is one a human in human form.

And Bhagavan Shiva gives the teaching that the minimum requirement for getting mukti/moksha is a human body. That is to say, no matter how difficult one's existential situation may be in life-- regardless of one's financial situation, whether or not one has food or a place to live, no matter what the situation -- if one has a human body, one has the minimum requirement. Where there is a tiivra iccha, a deep desire, one will learn sadhana and reach to the goal. That being said, for most people basic needs must be met before they will start to think about the Infinite Entity.  

When we speak of the impoverished people of the world we do so with deep sympathy and a determination to help them escape the claws of economic and social exploitation. And when we speak of the economically more established people of the world, there also we feel deep sympathy and a desire to help, but for a slightly different reason. Due to their samskaras as well as the miseducation by society, they have become slaves to their own indriyas. And there our duty is to try to help them to wake up and understand who they really are as human beings and what their purpose in life is.
