**Original Question:**  
Why does water expand when it freezes?

**Full Answer:**  
Water expands when it freezes because when water molecules transition from a liquid to a solid state, they form a crystalline structure with hydrogen bonds that hold them further apart compared to their arrangement in liquid water, resulting in an increase in volume and a decrease in density; essentially, the ice lattice created by the hydrogen bonds forces the molecules to be spaced further apart than in liquid water.

Key points about water's unique behavior when freezing:

Hydrogen bonds:
The strong hydrogen bonds between water molecules are responsible for this unusual expansion behavior.

Crystal structure:
When water freezes, the molecules align in a hexagonal crystal lattice, which creates open spaces between them.

Density difference:
Due to this expansion, ice is less dense than liquid water, which is why ice floats.


Intermolecular bond strength is the primary determinant of whether a substance is in the solid, liquid, or gas phase. Weakest intermolecular forces lead to gases, intermediate bond strength leads to liquids, and strongest bonds result in solid phase.
Intermolecular distance is related to bond strength, but it does not solely determine the phase.
In the case of ice, the relatively strong hydrogen bonds lead to a solid phase, even though the molecules are more spread out than in liquid water.
