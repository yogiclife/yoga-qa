**Full Answer:**  
One thing I'll say here that may help you reply to his queries: you needn't apply both the principles (randomness and energy) to every example. In the river example it is moving from a state of high potential energy due to the elevation in the mountains, down to a state of low potential energy upon arriving to the ocean. We can understand this state of high energy potential by seeing that people use this potential energy for actual energy production. See below:

"At hydropower plants water flows through a pipe, or penstock, then pushes against and turns blades in a turbine that spin to power a generator to produce electricity."

This can only be done when water is flowing from a higher place to a lower place. It shows the energy potential of water when located at a higher elevation. Due to the force of gravity, water has higher energy when located a higher elevation. By flowing to a lower elevation it is moving to a lower energy state.

The above is not an example of randomness, but of energy. That is, this is an example of enthalpy not of entropy.

The examples for entropy are the perfume (preferring to spread throughout the room) or the room (going into a messy state).
