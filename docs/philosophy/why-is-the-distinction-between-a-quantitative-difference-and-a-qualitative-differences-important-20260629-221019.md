**Original Question:**  
Mahodaya, can you clarify if my understanding of why we discuss qualitative versus quantitative differences is correct? A qualitative difference implies that it is your dharma. So, someone who does not exhibit human dharma is not really acting like a human. A quantitative difference would not carry the same implication. 

For example, someone doing stupid things is not showing animal qualities, because intelligence is a quantitative difference. But someone pursuing finite objects is showing animal qualities because this is a quantitative difference.

**Full Answer:**  
In replying to your question, let me approach it from one step further back. Before even discussing the subject of dharma, we discuss whether human beings are animals. It is at this phase that we introduce the concept of a qualitative vs quantitative difference. And we use it to show that a qualitative difference is sufficient cause for justifying the creation of a separate category of jiiva, whereas a quantitative difference is insufficient cause. So if we are saying that human beings are different because they are "more intelligent" than animals, we cannot justify the creation of a separate category of jiiva i.e. separate jati or kingdom on this basis as there is a whole spectrum of intelligence within the animal kingdom-- and fitting into this spectrum of intelligence in the animal kingdom, if this is all we can say about how humans are different, then we would need to keep humans in the category of animals. 

But that is not the end of the story, right? There is a difference between humans and animals which is not merely quantitative but qualitative. And that means humans have a quality which is not present at all in any animal. Such a difference is sufficient cause for designating a separate category of living beings outside the animal kingdom, known as human beings. Qualitive difference justifies change in jati/kingdom, whereas quantitative difference does not.

Showing that human beings are not animals and why-- this is a critically important point, as it provides a basis for our discussion of dharma as well as for the entire subject of svatantra iccha and "humans in human form" vs "animals in human form".
