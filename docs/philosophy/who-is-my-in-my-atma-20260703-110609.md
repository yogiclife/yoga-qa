**Original Question:**  
In Chapter Four “Who Am I”, it is expressed that a human refers to their atma as “my atma” and to their body as “my body” and therefore cannot be either of these; rather, they are the owner of these two. So who is it that owns these two? It concludes that the owner is the mind, characterized principally of the mahattattva “I feeling”, and secondarily by the two other functional forms ahamtattva (“doer I”) and citta (“done I”).  

So a question has come that, just as one says “my atma” and “my body”, can’t a person also say “my mind”? (For example, “आज मेरा मन ठीक नहीं है”.) So if one says like this about one’s own mind, then who is saying that?


**Full Answer:**  
So the reply is that, whereas a person really is different from their body and their atma and really is the owner of these two, in the case of mind it is just a way of speaking; here one really is one’s mind and is not the owner of it. When one speaks about “my mind”, one is speaking about oneself. Whereas, when one speaks about “my atma” or “my body” the meaning is that one is the owner of them.

And also: When we say that a person is the "owner" of their atma, here "owner" does not mean "controller"; rather, it means simply that this atma i.e. this portion of shuddha purusa has been assigned to me.

Explanation:
This is extremely clear—and this is exactly how it is. This is because the doer is the mind. The atma (soul/witnessing consciousness) does absolutely nothing. It will never say, nor will it ever feel, "This is mine...". The atma never thinks, "Something belongs to me." Only the mind does this. The doer is the mind. Therefore, it is exactly as I have written. When someone says, "I have a mind to know (I feel like knowing)," they are actually saying, "I want to know."

Hence, saying "My mind is fine/not fine now" is merely a figure of speech; the real meaning is "I am fine/not fine." However, saying "My body, my hand, my eye, my foot"—this is not just a figure of speech; this is a reality. Similarly, "My atma" is also a reality. This is because no one experiences that the "I" who is speaking is the atma; the "I" who is speaking is the mind, and that very speaking mind is trying to become the atma through the practice of sadhana.
