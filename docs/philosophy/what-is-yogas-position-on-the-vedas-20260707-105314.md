**Original Question:**  
Clarification by acharya ji.

**Full Answer:**  
The Hindu religionists get into all sorts of discussions as to whether the vedas are pauruSheya or apauruSheya, meaning whether the vedas are written by humans or whether, like God, they were never "made" and are therefore anAdi and akAraNa (causeless). In yoga we don't engage in this discussion. The vedas were obviously written by people; therefore the only question remains as to what sort of authority to ascribe to the text.



- Yoga accepts individual shlokas of the vedas on the basis of their truth value. Yoga does not accept any shloka of the vedas as true just because it comes from the vedas; each shloka is examined on its merits and is accepted if it holds truth. 



- The four vedas are divided broadly into two parts: (1) the jinanakanda, and (2) the karmakanda. 



- Yoga accepts and embraces those shlokas of the jinanakanda which are correct and hold value for spiritual aspirants; there are many such shlokas in the jinanakanda. There are however some shlokas in the jinanakanda that do not hold truth and due to this are rejected. 



- Yoga does not accept the karmakanda, as the karmakanda consists of rituals--sacrifices, ceremonial rites, and so-called mantras intended to appease deities and achieve worldly desires (such as wealth, health, and rain).  



As everything in the vedas was written by human beings, it is natural that some shlokas contain mistakes or are misguided. But there are also many shlokas that hold great truths, are beautifully written, and contain timeless guidelines. These should be and are indeed accepted in yoga.
