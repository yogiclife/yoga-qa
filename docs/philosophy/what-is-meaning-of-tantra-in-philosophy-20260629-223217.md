**Original Question:**  
Mahodaya, paragraph 14 of the karapatram on 'How human beings should live in this world' uses the term 'Tantra', saying that tantra can accelerate the experience of reactions to actions. What is meaning of tantra in philosophy?

**Full Answer:**  
So this is a very good question, one which is deserving of an entire class session. I will answer you here in brief though, so that you get a general idea. The word "tantra" in India conjures up images of people who live at cremation grounds, eat raw meat in the middle of the night, and cast negative spells on others. Tantra is divided into two branches. One is that described above, for which the term is famous.

The other branch is "tantra yoga", which is actually what we practice. Everything you have learned since we met comes within the aegis of tantra yoga. The word "tantra" means here "to liberate the mind from dullness/crudity". "Tan" means जडता or crudity, dullness. And "tra" means त्राण or liberation. Tantra yoga is the practical science of liberating the mind from crudeness or dullness. In other words, it is the science of elevating the mind from animality to humanity to divinity. 

Integral to tantra yoga is embracing the spirit of struggle or fight. Liberation from animality involves a fight. As it says in the first karapatram, there is within every human being a duel between the animal dharma and the manava dharma within them. If they do not engage in the battle, they will lose and surrender to the animal nature within them which has been present for ages. The spirit of struggle in tantra is beautifully represented by the tandava dance given by Lord Shiva, which we practice daily before starting yogasanas. 

So this is the basic idea of tantra yoga-- the spirit of mental expansion with the goal of ultimate union with the Infinite Entity.
