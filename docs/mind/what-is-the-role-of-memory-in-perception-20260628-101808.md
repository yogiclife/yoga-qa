**Full Answer:**  
So your question is about memory, right? I will give you a brief overview, as it is quite an in-depth subject.

1. Short-term memory is located directly in the brain. The primary location in the brain has been identified by modern science as the hippocampus, which is in the temporal lobe of thee brain. 
2. Longer term memory from within this life is located in the citta (of the mind).
3. Memory of past lives is called the extra-cerebral memory, which is located in the causal mind, also located in the citta. 

If one saw a white cow two days ago, that memory is still fresh in the neurons of the brain and so if the ahamtattva wishes to recall it, the neurons of the brain send the image directly to the citta-- the image is projected by the neurons of the hippocampus onto the citta which takes the form of the white cow. If one saw the cow ten years ago however, the image is no longer stored in the neurons of the brain and so if the ahamtattva wishes to see it, it has to direct the citta to go into its memory banks and see if it can bring up the image of the cow from ten years back. If it can, then the image will be projected onto the citta just as if it had been done by the hippocampus, but in this case the image is getting projected from within the citta. And in the case of memory from past lives, there is a special stage of mind called the causal mind, also located in the citta, and it stores all the information from past lives. Children under five are frequently able to access the information from the life immediately prior as it is still quite fresh; after the age of five however it becomes inaccessible to people. Your explanation was overall very good, just you'd indicated that memory is used in creating dreams, but not in direct sense perception. I just wanted to clarify that in the interpretational component of direct perception, memory has an important role.
