**Original Question:**  
Since we do so many different and new tasks every day, how can we know before doing them whether that task will elevate the mind or pull it down.

**Full Answer:**  
Towards the end of this session, X’s question is meaningful and good. He was asking that since we do so many different and new tasks every day, how can we know before doing them whether that task will elevate the mind or pull it down. The answer to this is very straightforward. It is not as if we do thousands of brand-new things every day. Any task or action, even if we are doing it for the first time, falls into a certain category. The categories of actions are very limited. So, by categorizing the task that is about to be done, it will become clear what its impact on the mind will be. He has already learned a lot regarding Yogic Life, and he has also learned a lot from Yoga philosophy. That itself is the touchstone.

Most of the questions in his life will arise in a social context—meaning, whether he should spend time with a particular person or not, whether he should talk to someone or not, or whether he should go out somewhere to wander or not—these kinds of things come up. And before spending time, talking, or wandering, X ji can certainly estimate what kind of environment his mind will be placed in by doing so. And based on that, the decision can easily be made. Now, his example that "until Acharya ji told me, I didn't know whether oil was a good thing or not"—that is fine, but these kinds of things are very rare now, and if something does come up, he can ask. For instance, a month ago he asked me if sattu is fine or not, so I told him. But such things are very few now—he has learned a lot, and based on his conscience-informed intellect, he can certainly make the right decision as to which action will be beneficial for the mind and which will not.

X ji’s other question was that since anyone can start practicing Dharma at any time and attain liberation (mukti), then why not just keep committing sins throughout life, and then in old age, engage in spiritual practice (sadhana) and attain liberation anyway?

The answer to this was given to Ashok ji last week in the other Yogic Life teachers' WA group. Three points were placed there; here, for the kind of question X ji has asked, there is a fourth point as well.

The fourth point is: is committing sins joyful? The kind of life X ji is talking about—would any human being actually like it? Never. An animalistic life and a life of sin is only a path of sorrow. It amounts to living in a mental hell. It is not as if people will enjoy it very much. The way X ji is asking makes it seem as though he thinks that very animalistic, sinful life is the blissful one. But this is foolishness. On the other hand, we know that the moment one begins to practice Manava Dharma (human dharma), the experience of bliss begins. It is not that relief is obtained only upon attaining final liberation, and until then it is a lot of suffering. Not at all—as soon as a human being starts the path of dharma, the mind immediately becomes well and begins to experience bliss. So there is no benefit in becoming a sinner. Not even a little bit of benefit. For this reason, I didn't understand the logic behind X ji's question.

There are three more points related to this which I wrote to Ashok ji. You must have seen them, but still, I am placing them here again—

You explained quite rightly that it is better not to wait to begin spiritual sadhana until later in life. And you gave two reasons: (1) One may not live until old age; and, (2) By waiting until old age, one will lose the opportunity to serve others. Both these reasons are correct, and there is a third reason which is also very important: (3) Doing sadhana needs energy, strength, force—and that shakti is present in youth. If one starts sadhana in youth one will be able to do it the whole life. But starting in old age is difficult. The great majority of people cannot manage at that age to change their lifestyles and do what is needed to become successful in sadhana.
