**Original Question:**  
Apart from the practical aspect you mentioned about free will, are the following reasonable arguments against it? The proponents appear to agree that there is an appearance of free will. (i) The claim of no free will is, then, non-falsifiable. Any exhibition of free will can always be claimed as just an appearance. Since this claim is not falsifiable if it is actually false, it is just speculation that cannot be verified. (ii) In addition, since there is an appearance of free will, a position that there is, indeed, free will is a more parsimonious explanation.



**Full Answer:**  
Yoga: You can certainly try using it. I usually take the approach I mentioned above, as I feel it addresses the core issue. And I think it helps people to understand the actual reality that there are two completely different perspectives which coexist here-- that of the jiiva, and that of Shiva. It is just the same situation with the advaetavadiis--  the reply to their claim of jaganmithyA is that they really do not feel it to be the case in their heart. When they do yoga sadhana and get God Realization, then they will indeed understand and feel that the world is His kalpanA as they will have become one with Him. Until that time they need to accept that this world is real for them and that this reality has to be accepted as the objective world in which we live (and therefore the need to do seva, feed those who are starving, and stop exploiters from exploiting etc). The same principle applies to free will: it cannot be denied.
