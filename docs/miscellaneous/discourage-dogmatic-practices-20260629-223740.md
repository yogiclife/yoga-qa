**Original Question:**  
Clarification from Acharya ji

**Full Answer:**  
you wrote that people come from diff beliefs and philosophies and they do not want to leave it...so it would take time for them." Yes, all this is true. Or I should say, it may take time for them. Some may leave it quickly also, depending on the person. But my main point here was that this question of hers is a sensitive one, sensitive in how we reply to it. She asked what I described above about the pronunciation and the bhava, and you replied to her that although the pronunciation is important, the bhava is the main thing. And you expanded on it by listing several famous Hindu shlokas, sahasra nAma ityadi, saying in their support that the bhava is the main thing. So yes-- that is true if the mantra and its meaning are proper, such as in yogic life. But when a person is sitting in front of "Lakshmi Mata" saying dogmatic prayers, then it does not matter what the pronunciation or the bhava is, the entire practice is misguided. So whatever the students may ask, but we should not encourage them like this in their dogmatic practices. And indeed it is a sensitive matter, as I noted, so we need to do this in a psychological way. But going forward, it is important not to encourage the students in their dogmatic practices. Whatever they do they will do...but we should not express our support or encouragement for such things. Otherwise they will get the wrong idea. Whenever students ask dogmatic questions, a good approach is to respond about how one should approach it in yogic life, diverting the matter onto a positive, dharmic track.
