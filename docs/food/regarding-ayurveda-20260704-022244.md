**Full Answer:**  
I will not say that nothing of what they use works. They have some treatments that may bring positive result, especially the physical interventions they do using massage and application of heat for musculoskeletal conditions. And some of their herbal remedies such as ashwagandha may be effective in reducing inflammation. But then again, we don't really know as it has never been tested.



In modern medicine, rigorous testing goes on to confirm something works before it is sold. I am not an advocate of their treatment modalities as you know. We prefer that lifestyle modification be used wherever possible. But here the point is that medicines need to be proven to work as well as to be safe, before they can be sold.



The theories and treatments in ayurveda need to be put to similar rigorous testing to assess their truth value. Then only can we know whether a particular treatment works or not. It may be that a particular ayurvedic treatment is extremely effective. And a particular person may even like to say that all ayurvedic treatments are effective. Ok, if that is the case than those treatments will surely stand the test of a randomized controlled trial. The treatment should work. If it yields the results it claims to, then it will be accepted and embraced by the entire world. On the other hand, if it does not yield the results it claims to, then that treatment should be duly rejected. I have personally seen that large numbers of people who have lost most of their vision due to diabetes and other issues go to a very well reputed ayurvedic eye hospital in Kerala. Over the course of years of ayurvedic treatment, their eye sight does not improve. But they do not have any other option, so they keep going back again and again. This sort of holding out hope to a desperate population-- those who have lost their eyesight-- is not ethical. Treatments should be proven to work.



As a field, I do not have much respect for ayurveda or the doctors who practice it. This is due to (1) their conspicuous avoidance of trials to confirm their interventions work, and (2) their religious faith in their texts and the treatments given therein. 



You feel you benefited from ayurvedic treatment of allergies and asthma. Let us see the ayurvedic doctors do a trial in which 100 patients got the ayurvedic treatment and 100 patients got placebo. Let them prove that it actually works and there is statistical significance in the result. 



What I have found over the years is that they stand by their treatments in a religiously dogmatic fashion. It is similar to the nayayikas who continue to defend their ancient texts which claim that the eye sees by sending the indriya out to the object it wants to see, rather than that the visual information is conveyed to the eye via light waves. Modern science has proven definitively how the eye works; even then, the nayayikas continue to drum up excuse after excuse for defending their outdated theories. The question becomes: Are we seeking truth, or are we just seeking dogmatic justification for outdated ideas? For nayayikas it is the latter, and unfortunately, all too frequently with ayurveda it appears to be the same.
