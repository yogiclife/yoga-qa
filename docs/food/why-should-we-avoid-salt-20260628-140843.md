**Full Answer:**  
Here you have put forth two main points:

1) Salt is healthy when taken in the correct amount;
2) We are recommending not to take salt only because it is addictive, but there are lots of foods that are attractive because of their taste, so wouldn't all these good-tasting foods be addictive? 

Here is the reply:

- There is no nutritional requirement for added salt. All fruits, vegetables, grains, and beans contain sodium and chloride in the amounts needed by the body. So: no need whatsoever to add salt. 
- As a result of the above-given point, any amount of "added salt" is above and beyond what the body needs. Any amount of added salt is excessive, as the body is not designed for it.
- Added salt is not only not needed, but also it is harmful. There is no amount of added salt that is healthy. 
- The recommendations printed by health organizations for "daily added salt intake" are all fake and concocted. Their thought process regarding these recommendations is the same as that described regarding oil: They think that people are going to continue eating salt no matter what they recommend, as people like the taste. So in view of that, they try to come up with an amount to consume which may not cause harm. It is not that they are saying people need to add this amount of salt to their food, but that if they add "only this much", then it will not cause harm.
- However, their recommendations are extremely misleading as it makes people think that adding some salt is good. Whereas, there is no science that shows that adding a limited amount of salt is better than not adding it at all. The best is not to add it at all. 
- The reason that it is best not to add any salt is that salt increases the risk of numerous diseases which are enlisted here:

1. Hypertension (High Blood Pressure): The primary consequence, forcing the heart to work harder and damaging blood vessels. 
2. Cardiovascular Disease: Including heart attacks, heart failure, and coronary heart disease. 
3. Stroke: High blood pressure, a direct result of excess salt, is a major risk factor. 
4. Kidney Disease and Stones: Excessive sodium impairs the kidneys' ability to filter fluid and increases calcium excretion, leading to kidney stones. 
5. Gastric Cancer: Added salt is linked to an increased risk of stomach cancer. 
6. Left Ventricular Hypertrophy: Enlargement of the heart muscle. 
7. Metabolic Dysfunction-Associated Steatotic Liver Disease (MASLD): Frequently adding salt to food is associated with a 35% higher risk of fatty liver disease. 
8. Osteoporosis: Increased sodium causes higher calcium loss through urine, which can weaken bones. 
9. Meniere's Disease: A disorder of the inner ear that can lead to dizziness and hearing loss, often exacerbated by fluid retention from salt. 
10. Obesity: Linked to added sodium intake.  
11. Other Side Effects:
Fluid Retention (Edema): Causes swelling in hands, feet, and ankles.
Bloating: A common short-term effect of salt consumption.
Headaches: Often resulting from vascular changes.

- The more salt one adds, the greater the risk of the above diseases. 
- It is very true that salt is addictive, and this is one of the reasons not to use added salt in one's food. None of the foods naturally occurring are "addictive". Added sugar, salt, and oil are the main addictive components of food today. People are not addicted to bananas, grapes, apples, spinach, or any naturally occurring fruit, vegetable, grain, or bean. 
- And importantly, none of the naturally occurring whole foods are harmful. They are neither addictive, nor are they harmful. Whereas, sugar, salt and oil all three are both addictive and harmful.
