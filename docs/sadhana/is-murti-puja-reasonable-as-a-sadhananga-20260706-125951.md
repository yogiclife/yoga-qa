**Original Question:**  
NamaskaraH, mahodaya. I understand your point that the Divine is infinite and therefore cannot be properly represented in any finite form. I also understand the practical spiritual caution that a murti should not be mistaken for the ultimate Divine Reality to be attained. You have provided a very clear explanation for why murti puja is incompatible with brahmacharya.



My question is whether, while accepting these points, murti-puja may still be considered a valid sadhananga: a finite but useful support that helps make the mind sattvika for those of us who have not yet attained that state of brahmacharya.



I will be teaching this topic this week and am concerned about student questions related to it – such as the handout’s statement about temple idols belonging in museums – especially because I too go to temples and perform puja. I experience good vibrations then, and this helps my mind withdraw from materialism. In that sense, it seems similar to other sadhanangas where we interact with the finite, such as satsanga.



Could you clarify whether there is a flaw in my position? Thanks.



[The context is a clarification from acharya ji about why brahmacharya is not a justification for murti puja.]

**Full Answer:**  
Namaskar Ashok ji,



I appreciate your candor and openness in bringing this question. 



- We need to ask ourselves, what is the philosophical underpinning of murti puja? Is there a way to understand murti puja in such a way that it will help a person to move towards ideation on the infinite? 



You have given the answer to this very nicely in the introduction to your question by expressing that, "I understand your point that the Divine is infinite and therefore cannot be properly represented in any finite form. I also understand the practical spiritual caution that a murti should not be mistaken for the ultimate Divine Reality to be attained. You have provided a very clear explanation for why murti puja is incompatible with brahmacharya."



- I agree with you-- there really is no philosphical ground that we can stand on to make an approach using murti puja seem reasonable or based on rationality. 



- Murti pujakas always stand on a particular logic to justify their stance that murti puja is helpful. I was going to write this as an addendum to my response to Vidhatrii ji's student's query last week, but didn't get time. I'll present it here.



Murti pujakas give the following flow of logic:



1. It is very difficult/impossible to ideate on the infinite directly. 

2. So we do so indirectly.

3. Our approach -- murti puja -- is easy, very doable.

4. It takes us forward step by step, it is an approach that prepares us to be able to ideate on the infinite directly.

5. Later on we will, through the spiritual benefit we get from murti puja, become ready to do sadhana directly on the Infinite Entity.



The problem is that each of the above five suppositions are wrong. 



1. It is not impossible to ideate on the infinite directly. We do it twice a day in sadhana.  

2. Murti pujakas do not do so indirectly; their mind is not goaded at all toward the infinite. Rather, they are ideating on a finite entity which, due to its sthula svabhava being made of wood and stone, serves only to crudify the mind.  

3. Murti puja is easy, very doable: Surely, this is true. But that does not mean that it works.

4. "It takes us forward step by step, it is an approach that prepares us to be able to ideate on the infinite directly." -- If we look at the population of those engaged in murti puja, we see that they never start ideating directly on the infinite directly. Rather, they remain engaged in ritualistic and dogmatic activities up to the day of their passing, many of them thinking that going to die at the ganga will somehow give them self-realization.

5. "Later on we will, through the spiritual benefit we get from murti puja, become ready to do sadhana directly on the Infinite Entity." -- This never happens. Do you see people involved in murti puja graduating from that and engaging in sadhana? No one does this; they never switch over from murti puja to sadhana.



- So the problem is that, surely, murti puja may be easy to do, but the fact that it is easy to do does not mean that it works. And really, it doesn't: by seeing the lack of philosophical underpinning as well as the narrow-minded thought process and behavior of those involved in it, we can understand that it doesn't work. As the mind thinks, so it becomes. The murti is a finite thing-- thinking about that finite object cannot bring the mind toward the infinite. It doesn't make any sense, and indeed because of this, it cannot goad the mind toward the infinite. 



- I would hasten to add here that your own mind and its impressive workings is a beautiful exception to the rule which I have expressed above. The mindset of the vast majority of those involved in murti puja is closed, narrow, and not open to growth. Whereas your mind has a broadminded outlook and is growing beautifully. I would suggest that this growth you are experiencing is due to your deep involvement in sadhana, as well as in the sadhanangas and in yogic philosophy. And so your psychospiritual growth is due to the yogic life you follow, and not to murti puja-- it is happening despite and not because of murti puja. 



The proof of this lies in the observation that those involved in murti puja only, and not in the yogic life, their mind becomes more and more narrow with time. As a group, they are the supporters of jativada, and their conduct goes against the proverbial statement: Vasudhaeva Kutumbakam.



- Those who like murti puja like it because they have been doing it a long time, and not because it works. It doesn't make any sense philosophically (as you have agreed in your initial note) and so it cannot work. That's why only those who have been doing it since their childhood like it. For them it is the known path. But for those who didn't grow up with it and are rationally minded, such people will never ever by attracted by the logic given by murti pujakas. 



- No one has ever benefited from murti puja. No one's mind has ever become more sattvika by doing murti puja. On the contrary: doing murti puja brings the mind down and makes the mind crude-- simply because what they are ideating on is itself crude. The vast majority of those who engage in murti puja, their mind because crude because of it: they become susceptible to believing all sorts of dogmas and rituals-- things which a sadhaka would never have any interest in.



- You mentioned that murti puja is a finite but useful support that helps make the mind sattvika for those of us who have not yet attained that state of brahmacharya.



What is the approach to becoming able to do brahmacarya bhava? Engaging the mind in brahmacarya bhava. That is the way. Do it, practice it, and one will become more and more adept at feeling that bhava. One can never become more established in brahmacarya bhava by taking some other bhava, especially one which goes contrary to it in every way. For example: If one wishes to learn to play the piano, will watching cartoons ever help? No, never. The way to learn to play the piano is to practice the piano, not to watch cartoons. How could watching cartoons help one learn to play the piano? It couldn't, and it doesn't. Watching cartoons is something else, and playing the piano is something else. 



- You asked whether murti puja could be included among the sadhanangas. Sadhanangas are practices which yes prepare the mind and body for sadhana. But there is a rational underpinning in the case of each sadhananga, how it prepares the mind for sadhana. Each one is based in science and rationality. WFPB food makes the body more healthy and the mind more sharp. Asanas prepare the glands of the body to support deep concentration and make the body more flexibile for sitting comfortably in padmasana. Vyapaka shaoca makes the mind alert. Spending time with others engaged in sadhana, i.e. satsang, makes the mind more attracted to Parama Purusa and makes the mind more fertile ground for sadhana.



Murti puja does not have any effect in making the mind more fertile for sadhana. Rather, it turns the mind away from sadhana. After years of teaching people sadhana who are involved in murti puja and watching them leave sadhana behind in favor murti puja, it is very clear what the effect of murti puja is. It cannot, can never be said to be a sadhananga. The reasons for this are the three which I mentioned in my response to Vidhatrii ji: 1) It makes people think the murti is a representative of Parama Purusa, which is impossible; 2) It makes people think that one crude object of the universe has a higher density of consciousness in it than others, which is impossible; 3) It makes people think that a totally crude object made of ksititattva, the crudest stuff in the universe, is holy. That is not true, and it can never be true. So there is no philosophical ground upon which murti puja can stand. And that is why it is said: 



उत्तमो ब्रह्मसद्भावो मध्यमा ध्यानधारणा

जपस्तुतिः स्यादधमा मूर्तिपूजाऽधमाधमा ||
