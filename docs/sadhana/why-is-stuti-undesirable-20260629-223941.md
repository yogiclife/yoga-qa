**Original Question:**  
Mahodaya, paragraph 23 of the handout on _How should human beings live in this world_ faults stuti as flattery to get something from God. Students may raise the objection that most people don't praise God with that intention. They love God and rejoice in recollecting His glory. From what you said in class, I think your objection to this position is that God wants jagat hita. Rather than praise Him, He would like you to serve His children. Am I right in capturing the essence of your objection to stuti performed in the second manner?

**Full Answer:**  
I think we have covered the five types of bhakti together, right? Religion functions primarily (99%) through rajasikii bhakti in which the worshipper is requesting mundane things from the Supreme Being. The religions of the world are based on establishing a business relation with God. The worshipper gives something to God (be it in the form of rituals, donations, flattery, etc) and gets something from God in return. Stuti is done all over the world and is part of this rajasikii bhakti, this business relation with God in which the worshipper is seeking favors in the form of mundane worldly benefits.

Raganuga Bhakti and Ragatmika Bhakti, in which the sadhaka loves Parama Purusa either because by doing so he experiences bliss (raganuga bhakti) or so as to give Parama Purusa pleasure (ragatmika bhakti) -- these two higher forms of bhakti are found in those sincerely engaged in yogasadhana. It is virtually absent in religion. So if even the students say that most people don't praise God with the intention to get something in return, it is really not true. Worshippers in religion absolutely do it to get something in return; that is what religion is all about. Loving God and not wanting anything in return is a higher form of bhakti, called kevala bhakti, which is found in sincere sadhakas deeply involved in yoga sadhana.

[Follow up question: I think I now understand the perspective from which you are saying this, mahodaya. You are taking stuti from the perspective of rajasiki bhakti. Most people I know don’t do that, and so I was wondering about it.]

[Answer to follow up question] Yes Mahodaya-- 99% of the world population who engage in any form of prayer or stuti, do so with the intent to get something.
